# Episode 047: Decanting & Aeration (Waking Up the Wine - Final Prep)

## [WSET L1 Practice Question]

Q. What is the primary reason for transferring an aged red wine into a decanter just before serving it?

A. To quickly increase the temperature of the wine.
B. To remove the sediment that has accumulated at the bottom of the bottle and serve only clear wine.
C. To mix sugar into the wine.
D. To evaporate the alcohol from the wine.

---

## [Answer & Explanation]

Answer: B. To remove the sediment that has accumulated at the bottom of the bottle and serve only clear wine.

Explanation: During the aging process of old vintage red wines, pigments and tannins combine to form natural sediment.

Drinking this as is can be unpleasant and rough in texture, so professional service is needed to carefully transfer the wine into a decanter to filter out the dregs.

In contrast, the main purpose for young wines is aeration to expose them to oxygen and make the aroma richer.

---

## [Expert Concept - Final Prep]

These are two ways to wake up the wine:

1.  Decanting: A sophisticated task to filter out sediment. Since old wines can quickly lose their aroma when in contact with too much oxygen, it is best to decant them just before serving.

2.  Aerating: The task of injecting oxygen. By contacting young red wines that have strong tannins and closed aromas with air, it makes the taste softer and the aroma explode.

3.  Process: Use a wide-shouldered decanter or carafe to pour the wine slowly. Once the sediment reaches the neck of the bottle, stop immediately.

---

## [Exam Tip & Trap]

[Trap]: Avoid thinking that "all wines are better if decanted."
Most white wines or light-style Rosé wines do not need decanting and may actually lose their fresh aromas.
[Tip]: In an exam, connect 'Aged Red' with 'Sediment' and 'Young Red' with 'Aeration'!
